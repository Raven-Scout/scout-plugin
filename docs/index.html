The problem

You generate more signal than any human can track.

Every Slack DM, calendar invite, PR review and offhand decision is real and time-sensitive — and it arrives faster than anyone can read. So you cope one of two ways, and both quietly cost you.

The completionist

Read everything, burn out

You try to keep up with every channel and inbox. Your attention degrades, the important thing drowns in the noise, and you still end the day unsure you caught it all.

The triager

Skim the top, miss the rest

You give up and read only what's loud. The quiet, critical signal — the buried contract clause, the blocked teammate — slips straight through, and resurfaces later as a fire.

Scout is the third option. Delegate everything that doesn't need your judgment to an agent that reads all of it, cross-checks it against itself, and reaches into your day only when something genuinely needs you. You stay the authority on what only you can know; it handles the rest — and it can read far more in a day than you ever could. That's the point, not a limitation.

Why it's different

Most assistants summarize. Scout corroborates.

Markdown memory, scheduled runs and self-improvement loops are table stakes now. Scout's difference is what it does before it tells you anything.

01

Cross-checked, not collected

No single tool is treated as the truth. A calendar invite is verified against the transcript, a Linear ticket against its PR, an email against Slack. And your own actions outrank everything — what you sent, merged or cancelled beats what a meeting note says you'll do. So the list you act on is the reconciled truth — not a dozen apps' worth of half-stories you have to merge in your head.

02

Confidence on every line

Every item carries its evidence level, so you always know how much weight to give it — and a one-source claim is never silently promoted to fact. Hover any tag:

verified single-source unverified stale contradicted
03

Disagreement is the signal

When your sources conflict, Scout doesn't pick a winner. It surfaces the contradiction with both sides laid out — because that conflict is usually the single most important thing in your day, and the thing every other tool hides.

04

A real knowledge graph

Typed people, projects, tasks and relationships you can query — not a flat pile of notes. So Scout actually understands how your world connects and gets the context right instead of guessing. Browsable in Obsidian as an interlinked graph.

On its own terms

It runs itself — and reaches you only when it's worth it.

Scout isn't something you operate. It works in the background on its own clock, doing more in a day than you could read — and stays out of your way until it genuinely needs you.

01

Works while you don't

Scout runs unattended, around the clock, accruing knowledge faster than you can read it. You stay the authority on the things only you know; it handles everything else, autonomously.

02

Asks only when it matters

When Scout needs your judgment it messages you and moves on — picking up your reply on its next run. Conversations are slow, parallel, and never block your day. No notification firehose, no babysitting.

03

Extends and rewrites itself

It proposes new routines when it spots a pattern, reads its own mistakes, and edits its own instructions to stop repeating them — every change a labelled, reversible git commit you can read and undo.

04

Forgets on purpose

Every evening it lets a few things that no longer matter fall away. An agent that only ever accumulates becomes a worse partner over time; deliberate decay keeps the signal sharp and the briefing honest.

Yours, end to end

Runs on your machine. In plain files you own. Tied to no one tool.

Scout isn't a service you upload your life to. It's an agent that runs locally and writes to a folder on your own disk — there is no Scout cloud.

Nothing new leaves your computer

Scout reads the tools you've already connected to Claude and writes to local files. No Scout server ever sees your data — there isn't one to see it.

Your memory is plain markdown

The whole knowledge base is human-readable .md files with [[wikilinks]]. Open it in Obsidian, grep it, edit it, or walk away with it — markdown is canonical, everything else is just a view.

You can audit and undo anything

Because it's all in git, every change Scout makes is a commit you can read and git revert — including the changes it makes to itself. Nothing about the agent's mind is opaque to you.

Not locked to one tool or model

Scout runs on Claude Code today, but the harness is a transport — not a cage. Markdown, git and your own connectors mean the agent's mind isn't trapped in any one product; the plumbing can change, your knowledge stays.

Questions

Good questions, honest answers.

Do I need Claude Code?+

Today, yes — Scout runs as scheduled Claude Code sessions on your own machine. But it's deliberately built so the harness is replaceable: markdown memory, git history and your own connectors mean Scout isn't permanently wedded to one tool.

How many tools and routines can it have?+

There's no fixed number of either. Scout uses any connector you've added to Claude — built-in, custom, or community — and runs any routine you define, on a schedule or triggered by real-world events. It'll even propose new routines of its own when it notices a pattern worth automating.

What does it cost to run?+

Scout itself is free and open source. It spends tokens on your own Claude subscription or API when sessions run. A built-in budget check skips runs once you're over your daily limit, and every session's cost is logged — so there are no surprises.

Do I have to use Obsidian?+

No. The knowledge base is just markdown with [[wikilinks]] — any editor works. Obsidian gives the nicest reading experience, a graph view of how people, projects and channels connect, but it's entirely optional.

How is this different from giving an AI “memory”?+

Memory remembers what it's told. Scout corroborates what it observes across your tools, gives it structure (typed people, projects, tasks), flags what it isn't sure of, and surfaces contradictions instead of guessing — and you can audit every change in git. Remembering is the easy part; the trust is the point.

Can my whole team use it?+

Each person runs their own Scout with their own knowledge base — it's built around individual context. Team knowledge still flows through your normal tools; Scout keeps each person on top of what matters to them.
