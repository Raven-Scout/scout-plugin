The problem

You get more signal than you can possibly track.

Every Slack DM, calendar invite, PR review and offhand decision is real and time-sensitive — and it arrives faster than anyone can read. So you cope one of two ways, and both quietly fail.

The completionist

Read everything, burn out

You try to keep up with every channel and inbox. Your attention degrades, and the one message that actually mattered drowns in the eleven that didn't.

The triager

Skim the top, miss the rest

You give up and read only what's loud. The quiet, critical signal — the buried contract clause, the blocked teammate — slips straight through.

Scout is the third option. Delegate everything that doesn't need your judgment to an agent that reads all of it, cross-checks it against itself, and reaches back into your day only when something genuinely needs you. You stay the authority on what only you can know; it handles the rest.

Why it's different

Most assistants summarize. Scout corroborates.

Markdown memory, scheduled runs and self-improvement loops are table stakes now. Scout's difference is what it does before it tells you anything.

01

Cross-checked, not collected

No single tool is treated as the truth. A calendar invite is verified against the transcript, a Linear ticket against its PR, an email against Slack. And your own actions outrank everything — what you sent, merged or cancelled beats what a meeting note says you'll do.

02

Confidence on every line

Every item carries its evidence level, so you always know how much weight to give it. Claims only one source supports are flagged — never silently promoted to fact.

verified single-source unverified stale contradicted
03

Disagreement is the signal

When your sources conflict, Scout doesn't pick a winner. It surfaces the contradiction with both sides laid out — because that conflict is usually the most important thing in your day.

04

A real knowledge graph

Typed people, projects, tasks and relationships you can query — not a flat pile of notes. A formal ontology underneath, browsable in Obsidian as an interlinked graph above.

How it works

Six sessions, one job each.

Scout runs as scheduled Claude Code sessions on your own machine and subscription — nothing leaves your computer that doesn't already.

07:00

Morning Briefingdaily

Full cold start: reads the whole knowledge base, queries every tool, cross-checks, and hands you today's action items.

12:30

Consolidation2× daily

Lightweight delta scans through the day — what changed, what's newly done, what needs reconciling.

15:00

Researchdaily

Goes outward — expands what Scout knows about the people, projects and topics in your knowledge base.

anytime

Workinteractive

Walks today's items one at a time, each with a drafted action you approve, skip, or edit. You stay the editor.

21:30

Dreamingnightly

Evening self-improvement: processes your feedback, deepens the knowledge base, and learns from its own mistakes.

Sundays

Meta Reviewweekly

A system-level audit — is Scout actually running well, and is it getting better over time?

It gets better

Most tools are no smarter on day 100 than day 1. Scout is.

Every night it reviews its own work. The knowledge base deepens, the mistakes get rarer, and the entire history stays in git — so the longer it runs, the more it's worth, and the harder it is to give up.

It keeps a record of its own mistakes

When Scout gets something wrong, the pattern goes into a mistake audit — root cause and fix encoded back into its own instructions, so the same error gets harder to repeat. It grows monotonically more reliable.

It forgets on purpose

Every evening it lets a few things that no longer matter fall away. An agent that only ever accumulates becomes a worse partner over time; deliberate decay keeps the signal sharp and the briefing honest.

Every change is in git

Scout's memory is a git repository. Every edit is a commit with a reason — so you can see exactly what changed, when, and why, and revert anything you disagree with. The history is the system's memory of itself.

Yours, end to end

Runs on your machine. On your subscription. In plain files you own.

Scout isn't a service you upload your life to. It's a plugin that runs locally and writes to a folder on your own disk — there is no Scout cloud.

Nothing new leaves your computer

Scout reads the tools you've already connected to Claude Code and writes to local files. No Scout server ever sees your data — there isn't one to see it.

Your memory is plain markdown

The whole knowledge base is human-readable .md files with [[wikilinks]]. Open it in Obsidian, grep it, edit it, or walk away with it — markdown is canonical, everything else is just a view.

You can audit and undo anything

Because it's all in git, every change Scout makes is a commit you can read and git revert. Nothing about the agent's memory is opaque to you.

You stay in control of the agent

When Scout improves its own behaviour, the change lands as a separate, labelled commit — loud and reversible, never a silent edit you can't trace.

Questions

Good questions, honest answers.

Do I need Claude Code?+

Yes. Scout is a Claude Code plugin and runs as scheduled Claude Code sessions on your own machine. If you already use Claude Code, you're most of the way there.

What does it cost to run?+

Scout itself is free and open source. It spends tokens on your own Claude subscription or API when sessions run. A built-in budget check skips runs once you're over your daily limit, and every session's cost is logged — so there are no surprises.

Do I have to use Obsidian?+

No. The knowledge base is just markdown with [[wikilinks]] — any editor works. Obsidian gives the nicest reading experience, a graph view of how people, projects and channels connect, but it's entirely optional.

What if I only have one or two tools connected?+

Still useful. Scout adapts its cross-checking to whatever you have — even a Calendar-only setup helps. More connectors simply mean more verification points behind each item.

How is this different from giving an AI “memory”?+

Memory remembers what it's told. Scout corroborates what it observes across your tools, gives it structure (typed people, projects, tasks), flags what it isn't sure of, and surfaces contradictions instead of guessing — and you can audit every change in git. Remembering is the easy part; the trust is the point.

Can my whole team use it?+

Each person runs their own Scout with their own knowledge base — it's built around individual context. Team knowledge still flows through your normal tools; Scout keeps each person on top of what matters to them.

Wake up tomorrow already briefed.

Install the plugin, run /scout-setup, and let Scout work the night shift. You'll get a short, cross-checked, confidence-tagged list of exactly what needs you.
